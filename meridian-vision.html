Vision

I'm 47. I feel like I'm just getting started.

The future is going to be extraordinary. AI is rewriting what's possible in science, in medicine, in how we understand ourselves. Gene therapies that were theoretical five years ago are entering human trials today. The tools to extend and improve human life are advancing faster than at any point in history.

I want to be around to see it. I want to participate in it. I want to help build it. And I want my daughter — she's six — to grow up in a world where her father is still making new memories with her, not a world where she's left with old ones.

One day, all we will have left is our memories.
And we might not even have those.

But today is not that day.

That's the optimism that drives everything I'm building. Not fear. Not a cold solution to a clinical problem. A bridge to the future — for me, for my family, and for anyone who believes the best years are still ahead.

But there's a gap between that future and today.

Last year I took a genetic test — not because anyone told me to, but because I was curious. It came back with a pathogenic variant in a gene called ATM. I'd never heard of it. My doctor hadn't tested for it. No guideline said he should.

That single finding changed everything I thought I knew about my health.

ATM means my DNA repair machinery is impaired. It means I have a three-fold elevated risk of pancreatic cancer — one of the deadliest cancers there is — and I should have been getting annual surveillance imaging that no one had ever mentioned. It means my daughter has a fifty percent chance of carrying the same variant. It means the statin my doctor casually suggested isn't just for cholesterol — for someone with my genetics, it activates a secondary DNA repair pathway that could matter more than the lipid lowering.

None of this was hidden. It was all there in the published research, in the clinical guidelines, waiting for someone to connect my data to the science. But my doctor had fifteen minutes, a basic lab panel, and no genetic data. He never had a chance.

I was doing everything right and flying blind at the same time. And it made me wonder — how many other people are in the same position?

The answer is: almost everyone.

I talked to seventeen men like me — founders, executives, physicians — high-performing people in their 40s and 50s who care deeply about their health and have the resources to do something about it.

Eighty percent were in physical pain or dealing with increasing injuries. Nearly all of them said the right team would help. Nearly all of them said they couldn't find that team — or couldn't afford the time to coordinate one.

These aren't people who lack motivation. They're people who lack a system. They see a PCP who knows one piece. A specialist who knows another. A wearable that tracks a third. No one holds the complete picture. No one connects the dots. And no one tells them what actually matters most.

The structure of American healthcare was built around a simple idea: your primary care physician is the quarterback. They hold the complete picture. They route you to the right specialist at the right time.

That model is broken and everyone knows it.

Your PCP has fifteen minutes. They see you once a year. They work from whatever you remember to mention. They don't have your genetic data. They don't review your wearable data. They run a basic panel of twenty biomarkers when eighty-five are commercially available.

This isn't a failure of competence. It's a failure of structure. The best PCP in the world, in a fifteen-minute visit, with a basic lab panel, cannot do what needs to be done.

Here is what I believe.

Artificial intelligence can hold the complete picture that no human physician can hold alone. Not because AI is smarter than a doctor — it isn't — but because it can hold genetics, bloodwork, wearable data, family history, and published research simultaneously and find the patterns that matter.

A single physician, equipped with this intelligence, gains superpowers. Not the power to replace cardiologists and oncologists, but the power to know when you need one — and why — before you or your current doctor ever would.

The value isn't in finding more things. It's in finding the right things and making them actionable. A list of twenty findings is noise. Three priorities, ranked by expected impact on your life, with clear next steps — that's medicine.

The specialist referral should be the exception, not the default. Most of what specialists do — interpret, advise, monitor — can be augmented by AI. What remains irreplaceable is their hands. Procedures, surgeries, physical examinations. Everything else is pattern recognition and guideline application, and AI is already better at holding the full context than any single specialist seeing you in isolation.

The practice of medicine is about to change fundamentally. Not in the way the last twenty years of "digital health" promised and failed to deliver — with patient portals and appointment reminders and wellness apps. But in the way that actually matters: the analytical function of the physician is becoming a computational problem. And computational problems get solved.

What Meridian does.

Meridian is a health intelligence platform. We collect four types of data that your PCP doesn't have: comprehensive genetics, expanded blood biomarkers, continuous wearable data, and structured personal history. We run that data through a multi-model AI analysis that synthesizes across every domain simultaneously — something no single physician, no matter how brilliant, can do in any number of fifteen-minute visits.

Then we give a physician the result. Not a raw data dump. A prioritized, ranked, clinically defensible set of recommendations with clear next steps. The physician reviews it, applies their judgment, catches what the model missed, and approves the plan.

The patient receives clarity. Not a wall of findings. Not an unranked list of suggestions. A small number of high-impact actions, explained in language that connects to their life — why this matters for you, specifically, right now.

We tested this on one patient first. Me.

The system found ten clinically significant findings. My PCP, through no fault of his own, would have caught three or four of them. The rest — the genetic cancer surveillance, the cardiovascular risk reclassification, the insight about my daughter — would have remained invisible indefinitely.

Within days, I was acting on recommendations I'd never received in years of annual physicals. Not because I was suddenly more motivated. Because for the first time, someone told me what actually mattered and why.

The future of medicine is going to be extraordinary. Meridian is the bridge to get there.

Gene therapies, epigenetic reprogramming, AI-powered diagnostics — the tools that will transform human health are coming. Some are already here. But none of them matter if you don't survive long enough to benefit from them.

Meridian exists to keep you in the game. To find what your doctor can't see, act on what matters most, and buy you the years that make the difference — the years between now and the medicine of the future.

I want to watch my daughter graduate. I want to walk her down the aisle. I want to meet her children. Those aren't metaphors. Those are the stakes. And I know now that the healthcare system I trusted wasn't equipped to keep me on that path. Not because it didn't care. Because it couldn't see.

I'm just getting started. And I don't want anyone else to find out late what they could have known early.

The Four Pillars

+

Genetics. A 167-gene panel that screens for hereditary cancer syndromes, cardiac conditions, and actionable variants. Your PCP will never order this.

Blood. 85+ biomarkers including Lp(a), ApoB, fasting insulin, and markers that standard panels miss. Twenty percent of the population has an elevated Lp(a) that their doctor has never tested for.

Wearables. Continuous heart rate, HRV, sleep architecture, and activity data. Not a snapshot — a film.

History. Structured family history, symptoms, medications, supplements, and lifestyle data. The context that makes everything else interpretable.

The AI Layer

+

Meridian's analysis isn't a single model making a single pass. It's a multi-model board review — multiple AI systems analyzing your data independently, then debating their findings adversarially. The result is stress-tested before a physician ever sees it.

The AI holds every relevant clinical guideline — NCCN, ACMG, AHA, USPSTF — simultaneously. No physician memorizes all of these. The system applies all of them to your specific data.

Then it ranks. Not just what could be done, but what produces the largest expected improvement in your life, given your specific risk profile. Three priorities. Clear next steps. One physician to execute.

The Physician

+

AI doesn't replace the doctor. It gives the doctor superpowers.

Meridian's physician reviews the AI's synthesis, applies clinical judgment, catches what the model missed, and approves the final plan. They deliver your results personally — not through a portal, not through a notification. A physician who knows your name, your data, and your priorities.

The specialist referral becomes the exception. When you need someone's hands — a procedure, a surgery, a physical exam — you see a specialist. Everything else, your empowered physician handles.

🧠 What makes your stroke risk different from your heart risk
Your CV card models coronary disease — plaque in heart arteries. Stroke is about your brain's blood supply, and you have a unique combination working against it:
💓
Unexplained bradycardia
Resting HR 45, nighttime dipping to 34 bpm on only 1.9×/wk training. Lower cardiac output = lower cerebral perfusion pressure. Your brain gets less blood flow baseline than it should.
🧍
Orthostatic hypotension
10+ years of lightheadedness on standing. Each episode is a transient cerebral hypoperfusion event — your brain briefly loses adequate blood flow. Chronic orthostatic hypotension carries 1.4–1.7× stroke risk.
🩸
Lp(a) → carotid plaque
Lp(a) at 72 nmol/L drives soft plaque in carotid and cerebral arteries — not just coronary. A carotid plaque rupture is how many strokes happen. Invisible to CAC scan.
🧬
ATM → impaired vascular repair
Cerebral blood vessels accumulate DNA damage from hemodynamic stress. Your ATM variant means that damage gets repaired slower — atherosclerosis in the brain, not just the heart.
✅ What's protecting you
Normal blood pressure (120/80) — this is the #1 stroke protector. Hypertension causes more strokes than any other factor, and you don't have it. This alone cuts stroke risk roughly in half vs. the average 47-year-old with even mild hypertension.

Fitness (VO₂ ~48) — 40-50% ischemic stroke reduction in high-fitness individuals. But this is declining (was 53 in October).

No diabetes, no atrial fibrillation — the two biggest stroke risk factors after hypertension, and you have neither.

How this refines your model
Once filled in, family history will sharpen every curve on this dashboard:
• Cancer: Family cancers on the ATM-carrier side compound your genetic risk estimates. A parent with pancreatic cancer would push your pancreatic HR from 2.8× toward 4-5×.
• Cardiovascular: First-degree relative with early heart disease adds ~1.5-2× independent of your genetic markers.
• Dementia: Parental dementia (especially before 65) is a strong independent predictor — and may hint at APOE4 status before you test.
• Bradycardia: Family history of pacemakers or rhythm issues would strengthen the case that your low HR is structural, not athletic.
